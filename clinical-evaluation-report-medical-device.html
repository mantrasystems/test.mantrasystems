Writing a Clinical Evaluation Report for The Medical Device Regulation MDR 2017/745

A Clinical Evaluation Report (CER) is an important technical document that must be produced for every medical device under the MDR. A CER details the process, findings and conclusions of clinical evaluation, and must be written in accordance with the rules for clinical evaluation in MDR Annex XIV Part A.

The MDR — Medical Device Regulation 2017/745, fully in force from May 2021 — replaces the Medical Device Directive (MDD) and imposes a wide range of new regulatory requirements. It introduces a new framework for the conduct of medical device clinical evaluation and the writing of CERs, including the need for a more thorough analysis of clinical evidence relating to a device. Writing a Clinical Evaluation Report for the Medical Device Regulation is a specialised task that greatly benefits from the involvement of an expertly-trained medical professional.

What are the requirements for producing a Clinical Evaluation Report under the Medical Device Regulation?

Article 61 of the Medical Device Regulation states that the clinical evaluation of each medical device shall be documented in a Clinical Evaluation Report that must form part of the technical documentation portfolio for the device. Annex XIV Part A expands upon this requirement and provides detailed guidance for performing a clinical evaluation, stating that the process must:

  • be based on a documented plan
  • identify all clinical data relevant to the medical device, whether favourable or not
  • appraise clinical data in a robust way
  • establish a plan for filling any gaps in the clinical evidence portfolio
  • reach objective conclusions about the safety and performance of the medical device

Although Annex XIV Part A outlines how to perform a clinical evaluation, it is comparatively light on requirements for writing a CER. It states only that “the results of the clinical evaluation shall be documented in a clinical evaluation report which shall support the assessment of the conformity of the device.”

What other information sources are available?

More detailed guidance on writing CERs can be found in MEDDEV 2.7/1 rev 4 “Clinical Evaluation: A guide for manufacturers and notified bodies”, an advisory document published by the European Commission. It provides a useful section on Clinical Evaluation Report structure but the guideline has not yet been updated to reflect changes in requirements introduced by the Medical Device Regulation. Because of this, adherence to MEDDEV 2.7/1 rev 4 alone will not be sufficient to achieve MDR compliance and manufacturers will need to consult externally for additional advice in order to ensure that MDR requirements have been met.

What happens to a CER after completion?

Following completion, a CER will be assessed as a component of the technical documents required by Annex II MDR to be submitted in support of the device. The procedure by which this assessment will take place will vary according to the risk classification of the device, with a greater degree of regulatory scrutiny being required for higher risk devices.

Manufacturers of Class IIa, IIb and III medical devices must submit the CER directly to a notified body for regulatory scrutiny as a pre-requisite for achieving permission to affix a CE-mark to the device. The MDR outlines responsibilities for notified bodies in performing assessments of clinical evaluation reports, requiring notified bodies to have access to individuals with sufficient scientific and technical expertise to make a reasoned assessment of the CER.
